Problem and Motivation

Challenge
  • Sentiment analysis for Algerian dialect is difficult because of code-switching, Arabizi, mixed scripts, and noisy social media text.
  • Centralized training is strong, but real institutions often cannot share raw user data because of privacy and governance constraints.
  • Federated learning allows collaborative model training while keeping raw data local.
Research Goal
  • Test whether DziriBERT can still perform well when fine-tuned in a federated setting.
  • Compare simple FL optimization methods on highly non-IID client partitions.
  • Measure the trade-off between privacy-friendly training and performance drop.

Dataset and Experimental Setup

Dataset
  • 13,260 X-platform comments, balanced binary sentiment labels.
  • 54.2% Latin-script, 38.4% Arabic script, 7.4% mixed script.
  • Split into 70% train, 10% validation, 20% test.
  • Train split partitioned into 10 highly non-IID clients using Dirichlet α = 0.5.
Protocol
  • 15 global rounds, 10 clients total, 5 sampled per round.
  • 1 local epoch per round, local batch size 8.
  • AdamW optimizer, learning rate 2e-5, gradient clipping 1.0.
  • Token length fixed to 128 for consistency across methods.

Why FedAvg?

Why choose it
  • FedAvg is the standard baseline in federated learning and is the most interpretable starting point.
  • It is simple to implement, widely cited, and gives a fair reference for future work.
  • For a first benchmark study in Algerian dialect FL, starting with the canonical algorithm is academically justified.
How it works
  • The server sends the current global model to selected clients.
  • Each client trains locally on its own data for one epoch.
  • The server averages client model parameters weighted by local sample counts.
  • Result: efficient and easy, but sensitive to client drift under non-IID data.
Core baseline explanation4 / 7

Why FedProx?

Why choose it
  • FedProx is a natural upgrade over FedAvg when clients are heterogeneous and non-IID.
  • The paper had severe client imbalance, from 4 to 5003 samples, so update drift was a serious risk.
  • FedProx directly targets that problem while keeping the same overall FL structure.
How it works
  • FedProx adds a proximal penalty to the local loss.
  • This penalty discourages each client model from moving too far away from the current global model.
  • That means more stable local updates and less divergence across heterogeneous clients.
  • In simple words: it keeps local training useful without letting each client over-specialize too much.
Why FedProx was the right comparison5 / 7

Why These Parameters?

α = 0.5Chosen to create meaningfully heterogeneous non-IID clients instead of easy near-IID partitions.
R = 15 roundsEnough to show convergence trends without making the benchmark too expensive.
m = 5 clients50% participation balances realism and stable aggregation each round.
LR = 2e-5Standard safe fine-tuning rate for transformer models to avoid destabilizing pretrained weights.
μ = 0.01Heuristic FedProx value: strong enough to regularize drift, small enough not to freeze learning.

These were reasonable benchmark parameters rather than fully optimized hyperparameters. The paper explicitly states that μ sweeps, α sweeps, and multi-seed evaluation are future work, so the contribution is a strong first benchmark, not a final tuned system.

Main takeaway
  • Centralized training remains best overall.
  • FedProx is the strongest federated method in this study.
  • FedProx reduces the gap to centralized training to less than 1 percentage point.
Interpretation
  • FedAvg is good as a baseline, but non-IID client drift hurts it.
  • FedProx improves stability under heterogeneous data.
  • Simple pseudo-labeling works, but there is a clear cost when only 20% of clients are labeled.
